FINANCIAL CONCEPTS — EVERYTHING IN THE MODEL
=============================================

This document explains every concept, variable, and formula used in the financial
model. It's based on the SPIO (Sales Process IO) framework by Nick Kozmin, combined
with standard corporate finance valuation theory.


================================================================================
SECTION 1: THE BIG PICTURE
================================================================================

The core thesis: for internet businesses, the fundamental value drivers are
marketing metrics. Everything upstream in the P&L is just arithmetic on top of
those numbers. The model maps:

  Marketing inputs (CPM, CTR, conversion rates, SDR activity)
       ↓
  Leads per day (per channel)
       ↓
  New customers per day (after time delays)
       ↓
  Revenue (booked and cash collected, with timing)
       ↓
  Costs (sales, fulfillment, marketing, fixed, transaction fees, refunds)
       ↓
  P&L (Gross Profit → EBITDA → Net Income)
       ↓
  Free Cash Flow
       ↓
  DCF Valuation → Enterprise Value → Equity Value → Share Price

No leaps of faith. Every number traces back to an observable, measurable input.


================================================================================
SECTION 2: STARTING STATE
================================================================================

cash_in_bank (cash_0)
  Cash you have at time t=0. The simulation starts here.

assets
  Other assets on the balance sheet (not used in core calculations, included
  for completeness).

liabilities
  Existing liabilities.

customer_count (c_0)
  How many paying customers you already have at t=0. If starting from scratch,
  this is 0. If modeling an existing business at a point in time, enter your
  current active customer count.

  Nick Kozmin tip: "You can model different states. If you're starting from 50
  customers, you can model from zero to 50 separately with different fixed costs,
  then create a separate model for 50+ and compare."

total_addressable_market (TAM)
  The total number of potential customers in your market. Currently used as a
  hard ceiling on active customers. (See known limitations section.)

upfront_investment_costs
  One-time costs to get started. Building the product, setting up infrastructure,
  etc. Example: $50K to build a SaaS product before you can charge $6,000/year.
  Deducted from cash balance at day 0.

debt
  Outstanding debt. Used to calculate interest expense and the equity bridge
  (Enterprise Value - Debt + Cash = Equity Value).

interest_rate (r_debt)
  Annual interest rate on the debt. Interest is charged daily as:
  daily_interest = debt × (r_debt / 100) / 365


================================================================================
SECTION 3: SALES
================================================================================

cost_to_sell (c_s)
  The total commission paid to close a sale, expressed as % of Price (P).
  Includes sales rep commission + sales manager override.
  
  Example: If the sales commission is 15% and the sales manager gets 3%, c_s = 18%.
  
  This is applied at the moment of sale, not spread over time.

time_to_sell (t_s)
  The sales cycle length in days. Time between a lead becoming qualified and
  the first purchase event.
  
  Nick Kozmin: "The sales cycle length is really dependent on cash flow. Getting
  to profitability from scratch depends on that sales cycle length."
  
  Example: A 14-day sales cycle is typical for mid-market B2B.

avg_deals_per_rep_per_month
  How many deals each sales rep closes per month. Used for capacity planning
  (not directly in the simulation math, but useful as a reference metric).


================================================================================
SECTION 4: MARKETING CHANNELS
================================================================================

The model supports four customer acquisition channels. Each can be toggled on/off.
The total delay from first impression to paying customer is:
  total_delay = time_to_market + time_to_sell

This delay is critical because it determines how long it takes for marketing
spend to convert into cash.

--- INBOUND (Paid Advertising) ---

The inbound funnel:
  Media Spend → Impressions → Clicks → Leads → Customers

media_spend
  Monthly budget for paid advertising (Facebook, Google, LinkedIn, etc.).

cpm (Cost Per Mille / Cost Per Thousand Impressions)
  How much it costs to show your ad 1,000 times. Found in your ad platform's
  analytics. Varies wildly by:
  - Geography (US is expensive, SE Asia is cheap)
  - Industry competitiveness
  - Audience specificity
  - Time of year
  
  Typical ranges: $5-15 (broad consumer), $30-80 (B2B professional), $80-150+ (finance/legal)

ctr (Click-Through Rate)
  Percentage of impressions that result in a click. Found in ad analytics.
  Typical: 0.5% - 2% for display/social ads.

  Derived metric: Cost Per Click (CPC) = CPM / (CTR × 10)
  Example: CPM $55, CTR 1% → CPC = $55 / (0.01 × 10) = $5.50

funnel_conversion_rate
  Percentage of clicks that become a lead. A "lead" = someone who completes your
  funnel action (quiz, form, booking page, etc.).
  
  Nick Kozmin: "If your funnel is done well, that lead rate will be around 2%."
  
  This is the first major leverage point. A well-designed funnel doubles this easily.

time_to_market_inbound
  Days between first ad impression and the lead event. For online advertising,
  this is typically 1-2 days (leads can come in almost instantly).

lead_conversion_rate_inbound
  Percentage of inbound leads that become paying customers.
  
  IMPORTANT: This is NOT the close rate of the demo/pitch. This is the end-to-end
  rate from lead → qualified → pitched → closed.
  
  Nick Kozmin: "Even if your close rate is 25%, not all leads turn into qualified
  pitches. Maybe only 1/3 turn into qualified appointments. 25% / 3 ≈ 8%."
  
  Formula: lead_conversion_rate = close_rate × qualification_rate
  Example: 25% close rate × 33% qualification rate = 8.3%

Inbound math (daily):
  impressions_per_day = (media_spend / cpm) × 1000 / 30
  clicks_per_day = impressions_per_day × (ctr / 100)
  leads_per_day = clicks_per_day × (funnel_conversion_rate / 100)
  new_customers_per_day = leads_per_day × (lead_conversion_rate / 100)
    (delayed by time_to_market_inbound + time_to_sell days)

Example with defaults:
  impressions/day = ($50,000 / $55) × 1000 / 30 = 30,303
  clicks/day = 30,303 × 0.01 = 303
  leads/day = 303 × 0.02 = 6.06
  customers/day = 6.06 × 0.08 = 0.48 (≈ 14.5/month)


--- OUTBOUND (Cold Prospecting) ---

The outbound funnel:
  SDRs → Contacts → Leads → Customers

outbound_salary
  Monthly salary/commission per SDR (Sales Development Rep). Usually $2,000-$3,000.
  This is a fixed marketing cost.

contacts_per_month
  Number of outreach touches per SDR per month. Includes emails, LinkedIn messages,
  cold calls. Typically around 2,000 for email-heavy outbound.

number_of_sdrs
  How many SDRs you have prospecting.

outbound_conversion_rate
  Percentage of contacts that become leads (i.e., book a discovery call).
  
  Nick Kozmin: "That number overall across everybody should be about 2%."
  
  A 2% contact-to-lead rate on 2,000 contacts = 40 leads/month per SDR.

time_to_market_outbound
  Days between the first message and when the prospect becomes a lead (books a call).
  This matters because if it takes 40 days to generate a lead, your ramp-up is slow.

lead_conversion_rate_outbound
  Same concept as inbound — the end-to-end rate from outbound lead → customer.

Outbound math (daily):
  contacts_per_day = (number_of_sdrs × contacts_per_month) / 30
  leads_per_day = contacts_per_day × (outbound_conversion_rate / 100)
  new_customers_per_day = leads_per_day × (lead_conversion_rate / 100)
    (delayed by time_to_market_outbound + time_to_sell days)


--- ORGANIC ---

organic_views_per_month
  Monthly impressions from unpaid sources (SEO, content, social, YouTube, etc.).

organic_view_to_lead_rate
  Percentage of organic views that become leads.

lead_to_customer_rate_organic
  Percentage of organic leads that become customers.

time_to_market_organic
  Days from first organic impression to lead event. Usually longer than paid (30+ days)
  because organic discovery is less targeted.

organic_cost_per_month
  The cost of producing organic content (writers, video production, SEO tools, etc.).


--- VIRAL ---

The viral loop:
  Active Customer → Invites → New Customers → (they invite too → exponential)

invites_per_customer
  Average number of referral requests or invites a customer sends during their
  active lifetime. Can be formal (referral program) or organic (word of mouth).

conversion_rate_per_invite
  Percentage of invites that convert to paying customers.

K value (viral coefficient)
  K = invites_per_customer × (conversion_rate_per_invite / 100)
  
  K < 1: Viral adds customers but growth is sublinear (each generation is smaller)
  K = 1: Each customer replaces themselves (steady state)
  K > 1: EXPONENTIAL GROWTH. Each customer brings in more than one new customer.
  
  Nick Kozmin: "If the K value is over one, the thing's gonna really jump up."
  
  Example: 20 invites × 10% conversion = K = 2.0 → explosive growth

viral_time
  Average days from invitation to close. If this is small (fast conversion),
  viral effects compound quickly. If large, the viral loop is slow/weak.

viral_start
  Day on which the viral mechanism begins. Maybe you launch the referral program
  on day 30 or day 100.

cost_to_sell_viral (c_s_viral)
  Commission to close a referred customer, as % of P. You still may need a
  salesperson to convert the referral.

cost_to_market_viral (c_m_viral)
  Referral bonus / incentive paid to the referring customer, as % of P.
  Example: If you pay $500 for a referral on a $5,000 deal, c_m_viral = 10%.
  If referrals are purely organic (word of mouth), this is 0%.


================================================================================
SECTION 5: PRODUCT & PRICING
================================================================================

price_of_offer (P)
  The average total price the customer pays over the contract period.
  
  Examples:
  - $100/month × 12-month contract → P = $1,200
  - $100/month × 3-month contract → P = $300
  - $100/month, no set contract → P = $100
  - $6,000/year upfront → P = $6,000
  
  Nick Kozmin: "A business is just a bunch of offers stacked together. This is
  offer-specific modeling. If you have multiple offers, you can stack these models."

realization_rate (RR)
  The percentage of billed revenue that actually gets collected as cash.
  Accounts for payment defaults, failed charges, disputed payments.
  
  Formula: RR = Cash Collected / Cash Requested
  
  Typical: 93% - 99%. Lower for payment plans, higher for upfront payment.

cost_to_fulfill (c_f)
  Cost to deliver the product/service, expressed as % of P.
  
  Nick Kozmin: "I like baking in the support into the cost to fulfill. Not just
  the gross contribution — bake in support people too."
  
  Typical ranges:
  - SaaS: 5-10% (mostly servers and support)
  - Online service business: 10-15%
  - Service/agency business: 25-35%
  - Physical product: 40-60%

time_to_collect (t_c)
  Days between the first purchase event and when you've collected all the money.
  
  THIS IS DIFFERENT FROM CONTRACT LENGTH.
  
  Examples:
  - Paid in full upfront → t_c = 1 day
  - 3 monthly payments → t_c = 60 days
  - 12 monthly payments → t_c = 365 days
  
  Nick Kozmin: "If you're selling upfront deals or getting the contract in two
  or three monthly payments, if it's in two payments: t_c = 30 days.
  If three payments: t_c = 60 days."
  
  The cash is distributed evenly over the t_c period in the simulation.

refund_period
  If a customer requests a refund, this is how many days after purchase it happens.
  Example: 60-day money-back guarantee → refund_period = 60.

refund_rate
  Percentage of customers who request and receive a refund.
  Example: 2% refund rate means 2 out of 100 customers get refunded.
  Refunded customers are removed from the active count and the revenue is reversed.

contract_length (time_to_renew)
  Days between first purchase and when the renewal decision happens.
  - Annual contract → 365 days
  - Monthly subscription → 30 days
  - One-time purchase (no renewal) → set churn_rate to 100%


================================================================================
SECTION 6: RENEWALS & CHURN
================================================================================

churn_rate
  Percentage of customers who do NOT renew when their contract expires.
  
  - 100% churn = one-time purchase (no renewals)
  - 50% churn = half the customers renew
  - 0% churn = everyone renews (ideal but unrealistic)
  
  Nick Kozmin: "If you're selling a one-time offer, this churn rate will be 100%
  and all of the other renewal stuff goes away."

price_of_renewal (p_renewal)
  What you charge for the renewal. Can be:
  - Same as original (P = p_renewal)
  - Higher (upsell/expansion)
  - Lower (loyalty discount)

cost_to_sell_renewal (c_s_renewal)
  Commission or cost to sell the renewal, as % of renewal price.
  If automatic (no salesperson needed), this can be 0%.
  If a customer success person is involved, maybe 5-10%.

cost_to_fulfill_renewal (c_f_renewal)
  Cost to deliver the renewed product/service, as % of renewal price.
  Usually similar to the original c_f.

time_to_collect_renewal (t_c_renewal)
  Days to collect renewal payment. May differ from original (renewals might
  be monthly while original was upfront).

renewal_rate_of_renewals
  Of the customers who renewed once, what % renew again?
  
  This creates a perpetual renewal chain:
  - First purchase: 100% of customers
  - First renewal: (1 - churn_rate) × (1 - refund_rate)
  - Second renewal: first_renewal × renewal_rate_of_renewals
  - Third renewal: second_renewal × renewal_rate_of_renewals
  - ... (geometric series)
  
  Example: churn 50%, refund 2%, renewal_of_renewal 50%
  - 100 customers buy
  - 49 renew (50% × 98%)
  - 24.5 renew again (49 × 50%)
  - 12.25 renew again, etc.


================================================================================
SECTION 7: ADMINISTRATION
================================================================================

transaction_fee (TF)
  Payment processor fee, applied to all cash collected.
  Stripe: 2.9% + $0.30 per transaction (we simplify to %).

fixed_costs_per_month (FC)
  Monthly overhead that doesn't change with sales volume:
  - Rent
  - Founder/engineer salaries
  - Tools and subscriptions
  - Insurance
  
  Nick Kozmin: "I don't like putting support people here. I like putting them in
  cost to fulfill. This is just rent, fixed salaries, engineers."

fixed_cost_increase_per_100_customers
  Semi-variable cost that scales with customer base. Per month, per 100 customers.
  Example: $500/month per 100 customers = you might need another support desk,
  another server, etc.
  
  Total fixed cost on day d = FC + (active_customers / 100) × this value


================================================================================
SECTION 8: VALUATION
================================================================================

--- General ---

tax_rate (r_tax)
  Corporate tax rate. Applied only to positive EBIT (no tax on losses).
  Lower tax rate → higher FCF → higher valuation.

inflation_rate
  Used for reference. The discount rate should already account for inflation.

time_max
  Total simulation period in days. This is the domain of the graphs.
  Default 2500 days ≈ 6.8 years.

number_of_shares
  Shares outstanding, as defined in shareholder agreement.
  Used to compute per-share price: share_price = equity_value / shares.

--- DCF Method (Discounted Cash Flow) ---

This is the primary valuation method. It calculates what the business is worth
based on the present value of all future cash flows it will generate.

Think of it as: "If I could see all the cash this business will ever produce,
and discount it back to today's dollars, what would I pay for it?"

projection_period_dcf
  The period over which we explicitly model cash flows (e.g., 5 years = 1825 days).
  After this, we assume steady-state growth via the terminal value.

discount_rate (r_discount)
  The rate of return an investor requires. Reflects the risk and opportunity cost.
  
  Higher discount rate → lower present value → lower valuation.
  
  Typical: 8-15% for private companies, 5-8% for established businesses.
  Often approximated as WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital).

perpetual_growth_rate (r_perpetual_growth)
  The expected long-term growth rate of the business after the projection period.
  Typically between inflation (2-3%) and GDP growth (4-5%).
  
  Must be LESS than the discount rate, or the terminal value goes to infinity.

The DCF calculation:

  1. Sum discounted daily FCF:
     PV(FCF) = Σ [FCF(d) / (1 + r_daily)^d]  for d = 0 to projection_period
     where r_daily = (1 + r_discount)^(1/365)

  2. Terminal Value (Gordon Growth Model):
     TV = FCF_annual_terminal × (1 + g) / (r - g)
     where:
       FCF_annual_terminal = average daily FCF in last year × 365
       g = perpetual_growth_rate
       r = discount_rate

  3. Present value of terminal value:
     PV(TV) = TV / (1 + r)^(projection_period / 365)

  4. Enterprise Value = PV(FCF) + PV(TV)

  5. Equity Value = Enterprise Value - Debt + Cash

  6. Share Price = Equity Value / Number of Shares

Nick Kozmin whiteboard explanation:
  "You're calculating the area under the FCF curve from T1 to T2, taking the
  present value. Then at the five-year mark you chop it and assign a perpetual
  growth rate. The terminal value is the cash flow at T2, with perpetual growth,
  present-valued back. Add that to the area. Then add on cash and subtract debt."

--- EBITDA Multiple Method ---

A simpler, "back of the envelope" valuation that uses market comparables.

enterprise_multiple_ebitda
  What multiple of EBITDA comparable companies trade at.
  Varies by industry. Can be found in NYU Stern's Damodaran database.
  
  Typical: 8-15x for software, 5-8x for services, 3-6x for manufacturing.

projection_period_ebitda
  The point in time at which you calculate the trailing 12-month EBITDA.
  Usually the same as the DCF projection period.

Calculation:
  Trailing 12M EBITDA = sum of daily EBITDA for last 365 days before projection point
  Enterprise Value = EBITDA × Multiple
  Equity Value = EV - Debt + Cash
  Share Price = Equity Value / Shares

--- Comparing the Two Methods ---

The DCF method is bottoms-up (based on YOUR cash flows with YOUR assumptions).
The EBITDA method is market-based (what would the market pay for this profile).

If DCF >> EBITDA multiple: The market is undervaluing businesses like yours,
  OR your growth assumptions are too aggressive.
If DCF << EBITDA multiple: Your cash flows don't support the market multiple,
  OR the market is overheated.


================================================================================
SECTION 9: KEY OUTPUT METRICS
================================================================================

time_to_profitability
  The day (and month) when cumulative free cash flow crosses zero.
  Before this point, the business is consuming cash. After, it's generating.
  
  Nick Kozmin: "The time to profitability is the intercept where the free cash
  flow crosses zero. Really important, especially as a bootstrap entrepreneur."

cash_consumption / cash_needed
  The maximum cash deficit during the simulation. This is how much additional
  funding you need to survive until profitability.
  
  Nick Kozmin: "The cash consumption is how much cash you need in addition to
  not run out of money. If we had $200K in the bank, that would be zero."

profit_per_customer_per_month
  (Trailing 30-day revenue - trailing 30-day costs) / active customers.
  
  Nick Kozmin: "I like comparing the profit per customer per month because the
  higher it is, the easier it is to get profitable and the shorter time to do so.
  You can compare offers — a high ticket offer with a long sales cycle vs a lower
  ticket offer with a short sales cycle."

K value
  Viral coefficient: invites_per_customer × (conversion_rate / 100).
  K > 1 = exponential growth. K < 1 = viral adds customers but decays.

CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)
  Total acquisition spend / total customers acquired.
  Blended across all channels, or calculated per channel.

LTV (Lifetime Value)
  Total expected revenue from a customer minus variable costs.
  Includes first purchase + expected renewals (geometric series).
  
  LTV = (P × RR × (1 - refund_rate) - P × c_f) + expected_renewal_value

LTV / CAC Ratio
  The fundamental unit economics test.
  - < 1: You lose money on every customer (broken)
  - 1-3: Marginal (might work with optimization)
  - 3+: Healthy (the "magic number" for SaaS)
  - 5+: Very strong, potentially under-investing in growth

Payback Period
  Days for a customer's revenue to cover the CAC.
  payback = CAC / (P × RR / time_to_collect)

SECTION 10: KNOWN LIMITATIONS & FUTURE IMPROVEMENTS
================================================================================

1. TAM is currently just a hard ceiling on active customers. It doesn't model:
   - Pool depletion for outbound (finite prospect list)
   - Recontact cycles with declining conversion rates
   - Audience saturation for inbound (CPM/CTR degradation)
   - Per-channel TAM

2. All marketing rates are constant. In reality:
   - Outbound response rates decline as you exhaust the list
   - Ad fatigue increases CPM and decreases CTR over time
   - Organic grows as you build content/authority

3. No seasonality or month-over-month variation.

4. No working capital modeling beyond cash collection timing.

5. No depreciation or amortization (EBITDA = EBIT in this model).

6. Single offer model. Multiple offers require running separate models
   and combining (as Nick suggests: "stack these models").

7. The simulation uses fractional customers (e.g., 0.48 customers/day).
   This is mathematically correct for expected values but doesn't model
   the discrete/stochastic nature of real customer acquisition.
