MODEL IN DAYS — PRODUCT ROADMAP
=================================

Four models. Each builds on the engine of the one before it.
Same simulation core, different lenses on the same business physics.


================================================================================
MODEL 1: LINEAR (current — shipped)
================================================================================

What it is:
  Single offer, constant-rate channels, day-by-day simulation from
  marketing metrics through to DCF equity value.

Who uses it:
  - Operators understanding their own business
  - Consultants diagnosing a prospect in the first meeting
  - Anyone learning how marketing metrics connect to valuation

What it does:
  - Inputs: channel config (outbound/inbound/organic/viral), product
    (price, fulfillment, collection timing), renewals/churn, costs, 
    valuation params
  - Simulation: daily loop computing customers, revenue, cash, costs, P&L
  - Outputs: KPIs (CAC, LTV, TTP, TTSF, CCC, margins), charts (FCF,
    cash balance, customers, revenue vs costs), DCF/EBITDA valuation,
    sensitivity analysis, GTM Alpha stress testing
  - Full calculation audit trail at any point in time

What's built:
  - Day-by-day simulation engine (2500 days)
  - TAM constraint (stops acquisition when market full)
  - Initial customers enter as retained (renewal economics)
  - Time cursor with full logic trace
  - Scenario comparison (A/B with delta)
  - GTM Alpha tab (stress-test single metrics)
  - URL-based model sharing
  - Monospace dark UI

Limitations:
  - All rates are constant (no saturation, no decay, no compounding)
  - Single channel at constant output forever
  - No events, no phasing, no channel sequencing
  - TAM is a hard cap, not a depletion curve


================================================================================
MODEL 2: GROWTH OPERATOR DEAL
================================================================================

What it is:
  Two-sided model showing the client's P&L and the growth operator's P&L
  from the same engagement. Answers: "What deal structure makes both
  sides profitable?"

Who uses it:
  - Growth consultants selling engagements
  - Agencies pricing their services
  - Clients evaluating whether to hire an operator
  - Both sides negotiating terms

How it works:
  Run Model 1 twice — once with current metrics (before operator),
  once with improved metrics (after operator). The delta between the
  two is the value created. A deal structure defines how that delta
  splits.

  Before: client's current reply rate, close rate, churn, etc.
  After:  operator's projected improvements to those metrics
  Delta:  the difference in revenue, FCF, and equity value

Deal structure inputs:
  - Revenue share (% of new revenue or total revenue)
  - Fixed monthly retainer
  - Performance bonus (triggered at milestones)
  - Equity component (% of equity value created)
  - Duration of engagement
  - Ramp period (operator takes X months to produce results)

Outputs — Client View:
  - Revenue before vs after
  - FCF before vs after
  - Equity value before vs after
  - Net gain after paying the operator
  - ROI on operator cost
  - Break-even month (when gains exceed operator fees)

Outputs — Operator View:
  - Monthly earnings (retainer + rev share + bonuses)
  - Total engagement value
  - Effective hourly rate
  - Portfolio modeling (if running N clients simultaneously)

Outputs — Deal Finder:
  - Given a target ROI for the client and a target income for the
    operator, what rev share % makes both sides profitable?
  - Sensitivity: how does the deal economics change if the operator
    only achieves 50% / 75% / 100% / 125% of projected improvements?

Key insight:
  The model shows that even at 20% rev share, if the operator moves
  reply rate from 0.15% to 0.5%, the client keeps 80% of millions
  in equity value creation. The operator's fee is tiny relative to
  the value created. This is the consulting sales pitch, quantified.

MODEL 3: REVENUE EVENTS OVER TIME (Live Valuation)
================================================================================

What it is:
  A timeline of campaigns, channel activations, and business events
  that play out over time. The model runs the full simulation with
  these events layered in, producing a live valuation curve that shows
  how enterprise value evolves as the business executes its plan.

Who uses it:
  - Operators planning their GTM roadmap
  - Investors evaluating a growth plan
  - Anyone asking "what happens to valuation when we launch X on day Y?"

How it works:
  Instead of constant-rate channels, the user defines a sequence of
  events on a timeline. Each event modifies the simulation state at
  a specific day:

  Event types:
  
  1. CHANNEL ACTIVATION
     "Start outbound on day 0 with 1 SDR"
     "Launch paid ads on day 90 with $10K/month"
     "Begin content publishing on day 180"
     "Activate referral program on day 270"
  
  2. CHANNEL SCALING
     "Add 2nd SDR on day 120"
     "Increase ad spend to $25K on day 180"
     "Hire content writer on day 200"
  
  3. METRIC SHIFT
     "Reply rate improves from 0.15% to 0.35% on day 60 (new angles)"
     "Churn drops from 10% to 7% on day 180 (product improvement)"
     "Close rate improves from 4% to 6% on day 90 (sales training)"
     "CPM drops from $55 to $35 on day 150 (new market)"
  
  4. CAMPAIGN EVENT
     "YouTube video published day 200, generates 50K views over 30 days"
     "PR feature on day 300, 100K impressions with 0.5% CTR"
     "Product Hunt launch day 150, 5K signups over 3 days"
     "Conference attendance day 400, 200 qualified leads over 5 days"
  
  5. BUSINESS EVENT
     "Hire COO on day 180, fixed costs +$8K/month"
     "Move office on day 365, fixed costs +$5K/month"
     "Raise $500K on day 200, cash_in_bank += $500K"
     "Price increase from $10K to $12K on day 300"

  Each event has:
  - trigger_day: when it activates
  - parameter: which input it modifies
  - value: what it changes to (or delta)
  - duration: how long the effect lasts (permanent or temporary)
  - decay: does the effect fade? (e.g., viral content decays)

Simulation:
  The daily loop checks for events at each day. When an event triggers,
  it modifies the relevant input parameters for that day forward.
  Multiple events can stack — outbound starts day 0, paid starts day 90,
  content starts day 180, reply rate improves day 60, etc.

  This turns the simulation from a static projection into a
  PLAN EXECUTION MODEL. You're not asking "what if my rates are X?"
  You're asking "what happens when I execute this specific sequence
  of actions?"

Outputs:
  - Live valuation curve: enterprise value at every day, showing how
    each event shifts the trajectory
  - Event impact attribution: "the paid ads launch on day 90 added
    $2.3M to EV by day 365"
  - Channel mix evolution chart: shows how the blend shifts over time
    (outbound dominant early → paid + content later → organic dominant
    at maturity)
  - Revenue quality curve: as channel mix shifts toward owned attention,
    revenue quality score improves
  - Plan vs baseline: compare the event-driven plan against a
    constant-rate baseline to see the value of the GTM roadmap

UI:
  - Timeline editor: visual timeline where you drag events onto a
    track (like a video editor or Gantt chart)
  - Or simpler: a table of events (day, type, parameter, value)
    that you can add/remove/reorder
  - The simulation reruns on every edit, showing the valuation curve
    update in real time

Key insight:
  Two businesses with the same inputs TODAY can have radically different
  valuations based on their PLANS. A business that plans to activate
  content on day 180 and referrals on day 365 is worth more than one
  that plans to stay on outbound forever, even if they look identical
  right now. This model captures that.

MODEL 4: CHANNEL DYNAMICS (the valuation engine)
================================================================================

What it is:
  Full channel-specific modeling with different physics per channel.
  Each channel has its own archetype (depletion, auction, asset,
  network, capacity) with saturation, decay, compounding, and
  interaction effects.

Who uses it:
  - Investors valuing internet businesses
  - Acquirers underwriting targets
  - Operators at scale optimizing channel allocation
  - Anyone asking "what is this business really worth given its
    attention engine composition?"

Channel archetypes:

  DEPLETION (Outbound / Cold Email):
  - Finite TAM with pool depletion
  - Recontact cycles with declining conversion
  - Infrastructure constraints (inboxes, domains, sending limits)
  - Deliverability as a state variable
  - List quality degradation (best prospects contacted first)

  AUCTION (Paid Ads):
  - Marginal CPA curve (CPA rises with spend)
  - Creative fatigue (CTR decays per creative)
  - Learning phase delays
  - Audience saturation
  - Efficient frontier detection

  ASSET (Content / SEO / YouTube):
  - Publishing cadence
  - Per-asset lifecycle (ramp → peak → long tail)
  - Portfolio compounding (authority effects)
  - Non-linear inflection dynamics
  - Permanent baseline lift from viral events

  NETWORK (Referrals / WOM):
  - Emergence threshold (needs installed base)
  - Satisfaction dependency
  - Network saturation
  - Trust-based conversion lift
  - Referred customer quality premium

  CAPACITY (Enterprise Sales):
  - Headcount with ramp periods
  - Quota capacity per ramped rep
  - Management span
  - Hiring and attrition lag

Channel interactions:
  - Content authority improves paid conversion
  - Brand awareness improves outbound response rates
  - Referrals only emerge from satisfied customers (retention feeds virality)
  - Channel mix affects blended CAC and reinvestment capacity

Cohort-level tracking:
  - Customers tagged by acquisition channel
  - Channel-specific retention curves
  - Revenue quality scoring by source
  - LTV varies by channel

Valuation:
  - Value attribution by channel: "35% of EV comes from content,
    32% from outbound, 20% from paid, 13% from referrals"
  - Terminal value reflects steady-state channel composition
  - Discount rate adjusted by channel durability
  - Revenue quality weighted DCF

Key insight:
  A dollar of organic-content-acquired revenue is worth more than a
  dollar of paid-ads-acquired revenue because the customer retains
  better, the acquisition cost doesn't recur, and the content asset
  continues producing attention for free. This model quantifies that
  difference and expresses it in enterprise value.

BUILD SEQUENCE
================================================================================

Model 1: DONE (current state)
  Linear, constant-rate, single offer

Model 2: NEXT
  Growth operator deal structure
  Estimated effort: moderate — runs Model 1 twice with deal overlay
  Highest immediate commercial value (selling consulting)

Model 3: AFTER MODEL 2
  Revenue events over time / live valuation
  Estimated effort: moderate — event system on top of Model 1
  Transforms the model from static projection to plan execution

Model 4: LAST
  Channel dynamics with archetypes
  Estimated effort: significant — new channel engine architecture
  The intellectual core, but requires the most engineering

Each model shares the same simulation engine, valuation layer, and UI
framework. The differences are in how inputs are structured and how
channels behave over time.


================================================================================
THE THESIS
================================================================================

The future of business valuation for internet companies is not:
  "What multiple should we slap on revenue?"

It is:
  "What attention engines does this business own, how durable are they,
  how efficient is the conversion, and what happens to the cost of
  attention over time?"

Model 1 teaches the basics.
Model 2 makes it commercially useful.
Model 3 makes it dynamic and plannable.
Model 4 makes it true.
