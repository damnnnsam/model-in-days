HOW TO USE THE FINANCIAL MODELING TOOL
======================================

Based on Nick Kozmin's 34-minute demo walkthrough of the SPIO model,
plus the capabilities added in this implementation.


================================================================================
GETTING STARTED
================================================================================

1. Start the app:
   cd financial_modeling
   python3 -m streamlit run app.py --server.headless true

2. Open http://localhost:8501 in your browser

3. The sidebar on the left has all inputs, organized into collapsible sections.
   The main area shows KPIs, charts, and valuations that update in real time
   as you change inputs.


================================================================================
STEP-BY-STEP: MODELING YOUR BUSINESS
================================================================================

Step 1: Starting State
  - If starting from scratch: cash_in_bank = your starting capital, customer_count = 0
  - If modeling an existing business: set customer_count to your current active customers,
    set cash_in_bank to current cash, set upfront_costs to 0

Step 2: Product / Pricing
  - Set the Price of Offer (P) to your contract value
  - Set Contract Length (how many days until renewal decision)
  - Set Time to Collect based on payment terms:
      Paid in full → 1 day
      3 monthly payments → 60 days
      Monthly over a year → 365 days
  - Set Cost to Fulfill (include support staff costs here, not in fixed costs)
  - Set Realization Rate (typically 93-99%)
  - Set Refund Rate and Refund Period

Step 3: Sales
  - Set Cost to Sell (sales commission as % of P)
  - Set Time to Sell (your sales cycle in days)

Step 4: Marketing Channels
  Enable whichever channels you use:
  
  Inbound:
    - Monthly media spend
    - CPM (from your ad platform analytics)
    - CTR (from your ad platform analytics)
    - Funnel conversion rate (clicks → leads, ~2% if well-built)
    - Lead → customer rate (remember: not close rate, but close rate × qualification rate)
  
  Outbound:
    - Number of SDRs
    - Contacts per month per SDR
    - SDR salary
    - Contact → lead rate (~2% is the benchmark)
    - Time to market outbound (days from first message to booked call)
    - Lead → customer rate

Step 5: Renewals
  - Churn rate (100% = one-time purchase, 50% = half renew)
  - If churn < 100%, set renewal pricing and costs
  - Set renewal-of-renewals rate (do customers who renewed once stay longer?)

Step 6: Administration
  - Fixed costs per month (rent, engineering, tools — NOT support people)
  - Transaction fees (2.9% for Stripe)
  - Fixed cost increase per 100 customers (scaling costs)
  - Upfront investment costs (building the product, setting up infrastructure)
  - Debt and interest rate if applicable

Step 7: Valuation
  - Tax rate for your jurisdiction
  - Discount rate (8-15% typical for private companies)
  - Perpetual growth rate (2-3%, must be less than discount rate)
  - Projection period (typically 5 years = 1825 days)
  - EBITDA multiple (look up your industry, 8-15x for software)
  - Number of shares


================================================================================
READING THE OUTPUTS
================================================================================

Top KPI Row:
  - Active Customers: current customer count at end of simulation
  - Monthly Revenue: trailing 30-day booked revenue
  - Monthly FCF: trailing 30-day free cash flow
  - Time to Profitability: months until cumulative FCF goes positive

Second Row:
  - CAC (Blended): all-channel average customer acquisition cost
  - LTV: expected lifetime value per customer
  - LTV/CAC: the magic ratio (want this > 3x)
  - Payback Period: days until a customer pays back their acquisition cost

Third Row:
  - Profit/Customer/Month: key metric for comparing offers
  - Cash Needed: how much funding you need to survive until profitable
  - Gross Margin, EBITDA Margin

Tabs:
  Valuation — DCF and EBITDA methods side by side, equity value waterfall
  Customers — Active/cumulative customer curves, new customers by channel
  Revenue & Cash — Revenue breakdown, cash collected vs costs
  Costs — Full cost breakdown (marketing, sales, fulfillment, fixed, fees, refunds)
  P&L & FCF — Gross profit, EBITDA, net income, free cash flow, cash balance
  Sensitivity — 2D heatmap showing how equity value changes with two variables
  Compare Scenarios — Save baseline, modify, overlay the two
  Export — Download inputs, monthly data, summary report


================================================================================
POWER MOVES: HOW TO USE THIS TO SELL / CONSULT
================================================================================

1. DIAGNOSE BROKEN UNIT ECONOMICS
   Plug in a prospect's real numbers. If the business doesn't work at their
   current metrics, you can show them exactly why on the cash flow chart.
   
   Nick Kozmin: "We developed this tool because we have a lot of customers who
   had offers but the unit economics really didn't work."

2. SHOW THE LEVERAGE POINTS
   Change one input at a time and watch the impact:
   - Drop outbound conversion from 2% to 0.5% → "the business breaks"
   - Move CPM from $55 to $100 → "affects cash flow significantly"
   - Change time to collect from 60 to 365 days → profitability goes from 2 to 6 months
   - Cut churn from 50% to 30% → watch the equity value jump
   
   Use the Sensitivity tab to visualize this as a heatmap.

3. BEFORE/AFTER SCENARIO
   Use the Compare Scenarios tab:
   a. Save current (bad) state as Scenario A
   b. Change the 2-3 metrics you'd improve for them in the sidebar
   c. Compare tab shows the delta: "+$4.2M equity value (+140%)"
   
   The delta is what you're selling. It's not "we'll improve your CTR" — it's
   "we'll add $4M to the value of your business."

4. COMPARE OFFERS
   Model Offer A ($3,000 product) and Offer B ($6,000 product) separately.
   Compare profit per customer per month. The higher this number, the faster
   you reach profitability.
   
   Nick Kozmin: "A $3,000 offer — that's not really going to work. It's just
   not likely to become a meaningful business."

5. MODEL DIFFERENT STAGES
   Create separate models for different business stages:
   - Stage 1: 0 to 50 customers (high upfront costs, scaling fixed costs)
   - Stage 2: 50+ customers (no upfront costs, different fixed cost structure)
   Compare them to see the trajectory.

6. SHARE WITH STAKEHOLDERS
   Use the Export tab to download:
   - Summary report (TXT) — clean text summary for investors
   - Monthly data (CSV) — for Excel analysis
   - Inputs (JSON) — save and reload later
   
   Or just share the screen in a meeting with the Streamlit app running live
   and adjust inputs together.


================================================================================
KEY INSIGHTS FROM NICK KOZMIN'S FRAMEWORK
================================================================================

1. "A business is just a bunch of offers stacked together."
   Model each offer separately. If you have multiple products, create separate
   models and combine the results.

2. "The profit per customer per month is a really important metric because it
   allows us to compare offers."
   High ticket with long sales cycle vs low ticket with short sales cycle —
   profit/customer/month normalizes the comparison.

3. "The K value is .2. If this K value is over one, the thing's gonna really
   jump up."
   The viral coefficient is the most powerful lever. K > 1 = exponential growth.
   Even K = 0.5 meaningfully reduces your CAC.

4. "How much does the churn rate really affect the value of the thing that
   we're building?"
   This is the core question the model answers. Every input has a quantified
   impact on equity value. No more guessing.

5. "The time to collect can really affect the business."
   Cash collection timing is as important as revenue. You can be profitable on
   paper and still run out of cash if collections are slow.

6. "We need a discount rate and we need an inflation rate."
   The DCF is the intellectually honest valuation method. EBITDA multiples are
   "back of the envelope" — useful for comparison but not as rigorous.

7. "This is offer-specific modeling, which is really really important."
   Don't mix offers in one model. One model = one offer with its specific
   pricing, costs, and conversion rates.


================================================================================
TYPICAL INPUT RANGES BY BUSINESS TYPE
================================================================================

SaaS Business:
  Price: $1,000 - $50,000/year
  Cost to Fulfill: 5-15%
  Cost to Sell: 10-20%
  Churn: 5-15% annual (monthly: 0.5-1.5%)
  LTV/CAC target: 3x+
  CPM: $30-80
  Funnel conversion: 2-5%

Service/Agency Business:
  Price: $3,000 - $20,000/project
  Cost to Fulfill: 25-40%
  Cost to Sell: 10-15%
  Churn: 30-60%
  Time to Collect: 30-90 days

E-commerce / Info Products:
  Price: $47 - $2,000
  Cost to Fulfill: 5-30%
  Cost to Sell: 0-5% (often self-serve)
  CPM: $10-40
  Funnel conversion: 1-3%

High-Ticket Consulting:
  Price: $5,000 - $100,000+
  Cost to Fulfill: 20-35%
  Cost to Sell: 15-25%
  Time to Sell: 14-60 days
  Outbound conversion: 1-3%


================================================================================
TROUBLESHOOTING
================================================================================

"The business never reaches profitability"
  → Price is too low relative to costs
  → Churn is too high
  → CAC is too high (CPM too expensive, conversion rates too low)
  → Fixed costs are too high for the revenue level
  → Time to collect is too long (cash timing issue)

"Cash needed is huge"
  → You're spending on marketing before collecting revenue
  → Reduce media spend or switch to performance-based (commission only)
  → Shorten time to collect (get paid upfront)
  → Reduce upfront investment costs

"Equity value is negative"
  → The business destroys value — costs exceed revenue over the projection period
  → Fix unit economics first, then worry about growth

"LTV/CAC is below 1"
  → You lose money on every customer you acquire
  → Either increase LTV (higher price, lower churn, more renewals)
  → Or decrease CAC (cheaper channels, better conversion rates)
